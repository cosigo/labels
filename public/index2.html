A malicious designation carries serious reputational weight. If a verified public contract is labeled malicious, any party applying or surfacing that label should identify exact technical basis and exact remediation path.

Centralization warnings are not malicious behavior

Scanner systems may identify centralization, owner-control, or market-control warnings. Those warnings may provide useful information. But they are not automatically proof of malicious behavior.

CIGO does not dispute that visible administrative controls exist where they appear in verified source code. Question is whether a specific control is actually malicious, excessive, or unsafe — and if so, which one.

Comparable administrative and market-integrity controls exist across many major token ecosystems and protocols people trade daily: emergency pause powers, allowlist or denylist logic, fee parameters, anti-abuse limits, treasury reserves, and other protective mechanisms. Existence of a control is not proof of malice. What matters is whether each control is disclosed, bounded, and used responsibly.

Calling a token malicious without identifying exact function, permission, rule, data source, transaction behavior, or remediation path converts risk review into opaque accusation.

What CIGO asks reviewers to identify

  • Specific source-code function being flagged.
  • Specific owner/admin permission considered unsafe.
  • Scanner rule or third-party data source behind warning.
  • Transaction behavior or liquidity condition being relied upon.
  • Exact remediation path required for reconsideration.

CertiK-style findings: warning categories versus malicious findings

CIGO’s position is that scanner-identified controls should be disclosed and reviewed specifically, not automatically converted into a malicious designation.

Naked token trap

Current token reputation environment creates a dangerous incentive. If a new issuer includes defensive controls, scanners may flag a token as risky or malicious. If an issuer removes those controls to satisfy scanner optics, that token becomes more exposed to liquidity extraction, bot activity, whale dominance, and predatory early-market behavior.

That is the naked token trap.

A “clean” token with no meaningful defenses may look better to automated systems, but newly pooled liquidity can be easier to drain, distort, or dominate. For an outsider launching a token, this can become a forced choice between scanner optics and holder protection.

What is truly dangerous is not existence of transparent defensive controls in a verified contract. Greater risk is pressuring new tokens into unprotected structures where liquidity can be extracted before ordinary holders have a fair chance to participate. After being labeled malicious without a clear technical basis, CIGO has no reason to weaken its own protections for opaque third-party labelers. Disclosed defensive controls will remain in place to protect liquidity, holders, and market integrity from predatory behavior.

Documented hostile launch environment

Research does not need to isolate every honest deployer loss to prove danger. Millions of measured sniping operations, one-day token lifetimes, and more than $103 million in slow-liquidity-drain losses already show that weak launch structures enter a hostile launch environment by default.

Public research shows that launch environment is highly predatory. One study identified 352,413 sniping operations on Ethereum and 1,716,917 on BNB Smart Chain. Another found that about 60% of tokens were active for less than one day. A separate 2025 study identified 3,117 slow-liquidity-drain pools with more than $103 million in cumulative losses.

Exact public statistics for honest deployers who self-funded a weakly defended launch and then lost all seeded liquidity do not appear to exist as a published category. That absence does not prove pattern is rare. It may simply mean this class of loss is poorly tracked, underreported, or buried inside a broader token market structure that rewards better-capitalized and better-positioned actors.

  • Industrial-scale sniping shows that new liquidity pools are actively hunted.
  • Extremely short token lifetimes show how quickly fragile launches can be overwhelmed or abandoned.
  • Slow liquidity-drain research shows that extraction is not limited to obvious one-step rug pulls.
  • Lack of a neat deployer-loss dataset does not make deployer-side liquidity loss unreal.
Conclusion is simple: scanner-friendly optics do not automatically create a safer launch. In a hostile launch environment, naked or weakly defended contracts can become easier targets for bots, dominant traders, and liquidity-drain strategies.

A hypothetical outsider launch pattern

  1. An outsider deploys a verified token contract.
  2. Outsider adds newly pooled liquidity.
  3. Contract includes defensive controls to protect pool and early holders.
  4. Wallets, scanners, or DEX risk feeds label token risky or malicious.
  5. Outsider asks for exact technical basis.
  6. Response is vague: “known risky/malicious features.”
  7. Outsider is pressured to remove protections without a clear remediation standard.
  8. If protections are removed, whales, bots, and predatory traders can attack pool more easily.
  9. Ordinary users see warnings before they ever review verified contract.
  10. Outsider loses trust, time, liquidity, and capital.
This is not a fair market test. It is a reputation chokepoint around permissionless infrastructure. CIGO’s position is that new tokens now enter a hostile launch environment. Scanner systems may punish visible defenses, while weakly defended launches remain exposed to sniping, whale dominance, liquidity extraction, and other predatory behavior. That combination creates pressure toward structurally fragile launches.

Market-access control layer

Blockchain may be public, but ordinary user confidence is filtered through private and semi-private interfaces: wallet warnings, scanner feeds, DEX risk labels, explorer token profiles, support desks, reputation APIs, liquidity optics, audit badges, and hidden remediation standards.

This creates a market-access control layer around tokens.

Established launch operators may already know how to design around scanner heuristics, wallet warnings, LP optics, metadata requirements, audit badges, and risk-feed expectations. Independent issuers often discover this only after deployment, after spending real money, and after their token has already been publicly labeled.

This is a club effect: not necessarily a written membership list, but a practical insider advantage created by opaque standards, private risk feeds, reputation systems, and interface-level control over user trust, access, and visibility. The highest probability of rug-style abuse can therefore exist within gatekeeping systems that control labels, reputation, and market access. That is where power is exercised most quietly: by shaping trust, restricting access, and imposing stigma without transparent standards, neutral review, or meaningful accountability. The least resistance to nefarious activity may exist among the same actors who control labels, reputation, and market access. In an opaque gatekeeping system, outsiders absorb stigma and restriction first, while insiders retain the practical advantage of visibility, trust, and freedom to operate. New market participants do not enter this environment on equal footing and may be locked out before any conventional “rug pull” could ever occur. As a result, outside issuers are unlikely to appear in the same abuse statistics at all, because they are filtered, stigmatized, or impaired before they ever reach equal market access.

Hypothecation hypothesis

Hypothecation means using an asset as collateral while ownership or exposure is reused through financial layers. Rehypothecation extends that logic further, allowing claims, collateral, and leverage to multiply beyond what ordinary users can easily see.

CIGO’s broader hypothesis is that token-casino economy often functions as a dilution machine: attention, liquidity, and wages are pulled into endless synthetic assets, while ordinary people are encouraged to chase digital claims instead of holding durable value.

In that environment, opaque labels become powerful. They can bless one token, bury another, steer liquidity, shape user confidence, and preserve same hierarchy that dominates legacy finance.

Public story is “permissionless markets.” Practical reality can look very different: permissionless contracts filtered through permissioned trust interfaces.

Dollar dilution and token casino

Deeper issue is not only one warning label on one token. Deeper issue is endless dilution of real purchasing power into speculative digital casinos, where new token claims, wrapped claims, synthetic claims, and reputation-filtered claims compete for liquidity while hard assets remain older monetary anchor.

In COSIGO’s view, gold and silver remain and reign supreme. They do not depend on a wallet interface, scanner feed, DEX label, or explorer profile to exist. They do not need a third-party API to confirm that they are real.

A system that can endlessly manufacture digital claims while privately controlling which claims receive trust is not neutral. It is a control structure. It can function like debt pressure relief for insiders and liquidity extraction for outsiders — erasing paper stress one hypothecated token at a time.

That is hypothesis. COSIGO will document evidence and let users decide for themselves. CIGO remains exposed to the market-facing side of that environment.

Questions MetaMask and third-party labelers should answer

Technical basis

  • Which source-code function is being treated as malicious?
  • Which permission is considered excessive?
  • Which transaction behavior is being relied upon as evidence of abuse?

Data source

  • Which scanner produced label?
  • Which data provider supplied warning?
  • Which rule, threshold, or scoring logic was applied?

Remediation

  • What exact change would be required for reconsideration?
  • What review standard would be used?
  • Who is accountable for correcting a false or unsupported designation?
If issue is centralization, say centralization. If issue is owner control, identify control. If issue is liquidity, identify standard. If issue is holder concentration, identify threshold. But do not call a verified public contract malicious while refusing to identify technical basis, evidence path, or remediation standard.
When no specific function, rule, evidence path, or remediation standard can be identified, malicious designation starts to resemble a guilty-before-proven-innocent verdict.

CIGO’s position

CIGO supports legitimate security review, public contract verification, disclosure of treasury wallets, disclosure of distribution wallets, disclosure of owner/admin wallets, disclosure of LP status, scanner findings, and contract controls.

CIGO does not support vague third-party labels that classify a verified public contract as malicious without identifying exact function, permission, transaction behavior, scanner rule, data source, or remediation path.

Transparent defensive controls are not malicious behavior.

A verified public contract can be inspected line by line. A vague malicious MetaMask label cannot.

Months of work went into building a verified and publicly inspectable token. At this stage, outcome no longer appears to depend only on code quality or contract transparency. Outcome appears to be shaped by a higher label chain made up of wallets, scanner feeds, risk providers, exchange interfaces, and support filters that can affect market treatment without giving a clear technical remediation path.

CIGO does not claim every actor in that chain is formally coordinated. CIGO does state that combined effect is powerful: a token can be verified, public, and inspectable on-chain, yet still be pushed into a reputational penalty box by opaque labels and vague support responses.

Latest MetaMask support response reinforced that concern. After repeated requests for a specific technical basis and remediation path, MetaMask stated: “I'm not here to tell you how to make a scam token appear safe.” MetaMask then ended further assistance on same matter and indicated future chat access would be removed for that issue.

Because of that, CIGO is not going to burn unlimited time and energy chasing an approval outcome that may never be available on transparent terms. Record will remain public. Evidence will remain public. Contract remains verified. Project can continue on its own terms rather than discarding a functioning token simply because label systems refuse to explain themselves clearly.
