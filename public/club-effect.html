Independent COSIGO public record. No affiliation with MetaMask, Consensys, Blockaid, CertiK, PancakeSwap, BscScan, BNB Chain, or any third-party scanner, wallet, exchange, or data provider. This page documents COSIGO’s position, observations, and evidence requests.

Club effect

Public blockchains may be open, but trust, visibility, and user confidence are filtered through a small, powerful group of private or semi-private scanners and interfaces. New participants are hit with toxic warning notices at deployment and treated as guilty before any fair review has occurred. That is prejudicial, anti-competitive, and imposed with near-total impunity. The real question is: who checks this small group of gatekeepers? Read further and examine the insider dynamics behind this system.

This page documents COSIGO’s position that token markets can operate through a practical insider advantage shaped by opaque standards, risk feeds, support filters, and reputation controls.

The chain may be open.
The contract may be public.
Trust can still be gated.

CIGO’s position is that the most powerful control layer is often not the contract itself, but the interface layer that shapes confidence, visibility, and access before users review the contract directly.

Market-access control layer

Blockchain may be public, but ordinary user confidence is filtered through private and semi-private interfaces: wallet warnings, scanner feeds, DEX risk labels, explorer token profiles, support desks, reputation APIs, liquidity optics, audit badges, and hidden remediation standards.

This creates a market-access control layer around tokens.

Established launch operators may already know how to design around scanner heuristics, wallet warnings, LP optics, metadata requirements, audit badges, and risk-feed expectations. Independent issuers often discover this only after deployment, after spending real money, and after their token has already been publicly labeled.

The least resistance to nefarious activity, and the greatest freedom to carry it out, may exist among anonymous or pseudonymous institutional actors who control labels, reputation, and market access. When decisions are delivered through pseudonymous chat forums, single-name accounts, initials, or partial surnames, the public may have no meaningful way to know who is speaking, who is deciding, or who is accountable.

This is a club effect: not necessarily a written membership list, but a practical insider advantage created by opaque standards, private risk feeds, reputation systems, and interface-level control over user trust, access, and visibility. The highest probability of rug-style abuse may therefore lie within the same gatekeeping systems that control labels, reputation, and market access. That is where power is exercised most quietly: by shaping trust, restricting access, and imposing stigma without transparent standards, neutral review, or meaningful accountability. It is prejudicial at its core. In such a system, outsiders receive derogatory labels and restrictions first, while insiders retain the practical advantage of visibility, trust, and freedom to operate with impunity, bnbpaperwallet.com is a perfect honeypot example, currently operational as theft mechanism (see snakepit.cosigo.io) . New market participants do not enter this environment on equal footing and may be locked out before any conventional “rug pull” could ever occur. As a result, outside issuers are unlikely to appear in the same abuse statistics at all, because they are filtered, stigmatized, or impaired before they ever reach equal market access.
